Candlestick Patterns: Complete Guide
— Basics to Expert

Everything you need to read the market's language — from understanding a single candle to spotting powerful multi-candle patterns, using AI tools, and practicing risk-free on a demo account.

🗓 2026 ⏱ 15 min read 📊 Beginner to Expert
⚠️ Educational Content Only: This guide is purely for learning purposes. Nothing here is financial advice. Trading involves significant risk — never invest money you cannot afford to lose. Always practice on a demo account before using real funds.

1. What Is a Candlestick?

If the market is a conversation, candlesticks are its words. Every single candle on a chart tells you a story — where the price started, where it went, how far it pushed, and where it finally closed. Once you learn to read them, you stop looking at random lines and start seeing real market psychology.

Candlestick charts were developed in Japan in the 1700s by rice traders — centuries before modern stock markets even existed. Today, they are the most widely used chart type by professional traders worldwide. Every trading platform, every broker, and every serious trader uses candlestick charts.

The beauty of candlesticks is their simplicity. In one small graphic, they pack four critical pieces of data: the open price, the close price, the high, and the low for any given time period.

Anatomy of a candlestick — the four key data points in one visual

The Anatomy of a Single Candle

Every candlestick has two main parts:

  • The Body: The wide rectangular part. It represents the range between the open and close price. A green (or white) body means the price closed higher than it opened — buyers won. A red (or black) body means the price closed lower — sellers won.
  • The Wicks (Shadows): The thin lines extending above and below the body. The top wick shows the highest price reached. The bottom wick shows the lowest price reached during that period.
💡

Quick Rule of Thumb

Long body = strong conviction. Short body = indecision. Long wicks = price was rejected at that level — the market didn't want to stay there.

2. Why Candlesticks Matter

Most beginners look at price and ask "is it going up or down?" Candlestick readers ask something deeper: "Who is in control — buyers or sellers — and is that control getting stronger or weaker?"

This distinction is everything. A price can be rising, but the candlesticks can be whispering that buyers are losing energy. A reversal is coming — and candlestick patterns will often show you before the price confirms it.

Professional traders use candlestick patterns alongside volume, support and resistance levels, and trend analysis to make high-probability decisions. No pattern works 100% of the time — but understanding them gives you a significant edge over random entry and exit decisions.

⚠️

The Most Common Beginner Mistake

Trading based on a single pattern alone. Candlestick patterns are signals, not guarantees. Always confirm with at least one more indicator — volume, trend direction, or a key price level.

3. Basic Candlestick Patterns

Let's start with the foundational patterns every trader must know. These are single-candle patterns — they appear frequently and are the building blocks for everything more advanced.

Common bullish reversal patterns — green signals potential upward movement

🔨
Hammer
Bullish

Small body at top, long lower wick. Sellers pushed price down hard but buyers fought back and closed near the open. Often signals a bottom.

⭐
Doji
Neutral

Open and close are almost equal. Neither buyers nor sellers won. Signals indecision — watch what happens next carefully.

🕯️
Marubozu
Strong Signal

Full body, no wicks. Whoever was in control (buyers or sellers) dominated the entire session without a fight. Very strong momentum signal.

🌠
Shooting Star
Bearish

Small body at bottom, long upper wick. Buyers tried to push price up but failed and sellers closed the price near the open. Signals a top.

🔄
Spinning Top
Neutral

Small body with equal-ish wicks on both sides. Market is uncertain. After a strong trend, this can signal exhaustion and potential reversal.

🪝
Inverted Hammer
Bullish

Small body at bottom, long upper wick. After a downtrend, buyers tried to take over. Needs confirmation on the next candle.

4. Advanced Multi-Candle Patterns

Single candles tell you a sentence. Multi-candle patterns tell you a paragraph. These are the patterns professional traders watch most closely — because they require coordination across multiple sessions to form, making them statistically more reliable.

Bearish reversal patterns — these signal potential downward moves after an uptrend

Bullish Reversal Patterns (Price May Go Up)

🌅
Morning Star
Bullish

3-candle pattern. Big red candle → small indecision candle → big green candle. Classic bottom reversal signal after a downtrend.

🤗
Bullish Engulfing
Bullish

A large green candle completely "engulfs" the previous red candle. Buyers took full control in one session. Strong reversal signal.

👶
Bullish Harami
Bullish

Small green candle forms inside a large red candle. The downtrend is losing momentum — indecision forming inside the previous move.

🌄
Piercing Line
Bullish

After a red candle, a green candle opens below but closes more than halfway up the red candle. Buyers are fighting back significantly.

Bearish Reversal Patterns (Price May Go Down)

🌆
Evening Star
Bearish

Opposite of Morning Star. Big green candle → small indecision → big red candle. Signals the top of an uptrend reversing down.

😤
Bearish Engulfing
Bearish

A large red candle swallows the previous green candle entirely. Sellers took complete control. Strong signal after an uptrend.

🌫️
Dark Cloud Cover
Bearish

Green candle followed by red candle opening higher but closing below the midpoint of the previous green candle. Sellers overpowering buyers.

🌙
Three Black Crows
Bearish

Three consecutive red candles each closing lower. Sustained selling pressure — a powerful downtrend confirmation pattern.

Continuation Patterns (Trend Continues)

Not all patterns signal reversals. These patterns appear during a trend and suggest the current direction will continue after a brief pause.

  • Three White Soldiers: Three consecutive green candles, each closing higher — strong bullish momentum continuing
  • Rising Three Methods: Long green candle, three small red ones, another long green — bullish trend pausing and resuming
  • Falling Three Methods: The bearish equivalent — downtrend pausing before continuing lower

5. How to Read Charts Like a Pro

Knowing individual patterns is only half the skill. Knowing where those patterns appear on a chart is what separates beginners from professionals.

The Three Questions to Ask Every Time

1

What is the trend?

Is price making higher highs and higher lows (uptrend)? Lower highs and lower lows (downtrend)? Or moving sideways (consolidation)? A bullish pattern in a downtrend carries far less weight than the same pattern at the end of a downtrend.

2

Where is price relative to support and resistance?

A hammer at a key support level is a high-probability signal. The same hammer in the middle of nowhere carries much less significance. Always identify key price levels first.

3

What is volume doing?

A bullish engulfing pattern with high volume is far stronger than the same pattern on low volume. Volume confirms conviction. No volume, no conviction.

Timeframes — Which One to Use?

Every candlestick represents a time period. A candle on a 1-hour chart shows one hour of price action. A candle on a daily chart shows one full day. Here's a general guide:

  • 1-minute / 5-minute: Scalping — very fast trades, high noise, not recommended for beginners
  • 15-minute / 1-hour: Day trading — good balance of signals and noise
  • 4-hour / Daily: Swing trading — best for beginners, cleaner signals, less stress
  • Weekly / Monthly: Position trading — long-term views, very reliable patterns
🎯

Pro Tip: Multi-Timeframe Analysis

Always check the higher timeframe first for trend direction, then drop to a lower timeframe to find your entry. A bullish signal on the 1-hour chart carries more weight if the 4-hour chart is also in an uptrend.

How to Use AI for Trading Education (Not Automation)

AI tools are powerful, but they are learning tools first. Here is how to use them smartly as a beginner:

  • Use TradingView's pattern recognition to check if you correctly identified a pattern — compare your reading to the AI's
  • Use ChatGPT or Claude to explain patterns to you in simple language, build study quizzes, or help you understand why a trade worked or failed
  • Use Tickeron to see AI confidence scores on patterns — learn what makes a pattern high or low quality
  • Never blindly follow AI signals — always understand the reasoning behind them first

AI Cannot Predict the Future

No AI tool can guarantee trading outcomes. Markets are influenced by news, geopolitics, and human emotion — factors that break patterns regularly. Use AI to accelerate learning, not to replace judgment.

How Long Should You Practice on Demo?

There is no fixed answer, but a good rule of thumb is this: do not switch to real money until you have been consistently profitable on demo for at least 3 months. One or two good weeks is not enough. Markets change. You need to prove to yourself that your edge works across different market conditions.

⚠️

The FOMO Trap — The Most Expensive Mistake

Seeing someone post profits online and immediately depositing money is how most new traders lose everything. Someone's highlight reel is not a trading education. Learn first. Practice second. Real money third — and only with money you can afford to lose completely.

8. Risk Management — The Skill Nobody Talks About Enough

You can identify every candlestick pattern perfectly and still lose all your money if you ignore risk management. This is what professional traders say separates survivors from those who blow up their accounts.

The Core Rules

  • Never risk more than 1–2% of your account on a single trade. If you have $1,000, risk $10–$20 per trade — not $200.
  • Always use a stop loss. A stop loss is an automatic exit order that limits your loss. Set it before you enter any trade.
  • Aim for at least a 1:2 risk-reward ratio. If you risk $10, you should be aiming to make at least $20 on the trade.
  • Do not average down on losing trades. If the market is going against you, do not add more money hoping it comes back.
  • Keep a trading journal. Track every trade — why you entered, what pattern you saw, what happened, and what you learned.

Trading Psychology — Your Biggest Enemy Is Yourself

Fear and greed are the two emotions that destroy traders. Fear makes you exit winning trades too early. Greed makes you hold losing trades too long hoping they recover. Recognizing these emotional patterns in yourself — the same way you recognize candlestick patterns on a chart — is what makes a trader truly advanced.

When you feel the urge to make a trade out of excitement or panic, step away from the screen. The market will always give you another opportunity. Your emotional capital is harder to recover than financial capital.
